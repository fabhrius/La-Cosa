## Prologue


Time.

It buries cities. Erases roads. Turns certainty into legend.

And even now, as we speak, it is slowly being consumed—like the oil in a lamp.


Some believe that time is a path already laid before us.

That the past, the present, and the future all exist at once, and that every event occupies a place that cannot be changed.

Others claim that only the present is real.

That the past survives only in memory, and that the future is nothing more than a collection of possibilities.


Time has fascinated mankind since the beginning of days.

The sages of old sought answers in the stars, in dreams, and in numbers.

Some legends say they even built strange instruments for the sole purpose of unveiling what was yet to come—destiny itself.


Those secrets have been lost to the passing centuries.

But...

What would you do if you could know the future?

&nbsp;

&nbsp;

What I am about to tell happened so long ago that no one remembers when.
In a place that no longer appears on any map.

In a small village called Drywind, north of the Flatlands, not far from Prosperous City.

There once stood an auction house there.

And this story begins exactly there.

## Chapter 1: Master Dahg's Auction House

::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 1 – The Shop
:::

::: {custom-style="ChapterStart"}
In a small village called Drywind, there once stood an auction house known as Master Dahg's Auction House.
The shop stood at the far end of the market road, just where the last houses of the village gave way to the open fields. 
:::

It was an old building of weathered wood, darkened by the passing years, with a long covered porch that creaked beneath the weight of every visitor.
Outside were displayed used tools, worn chests, and all manner of ordinary objects rescued from oblivion.

Inside lay a vast hall where sunlight barely managed to penetrate the narrow, dust-covered windows, leaving most of the room shrouded in shadow.

The wavering flames of candles and oil lamps gave the place an air of mystery, casting restless shadows across the walls and making the objects seem to move whenever they were not watched directly.

The air smelled of aged parchment, damp wood, and melted wax.
Antiquities from distant lands and forgotten ages crowded every corner.
Stuffed animals watched from the shelves with dull glass eyes. Strange copper instruments rested upon cluttered tables. Old weapons coated in rust hung from the beams overhead. Mirrors framed in ornate carvings returned dim reflections. Furniture engraved with incomprehensible symbols waited silently in the darkest corners.

Every object seemed to hold a story.
Memories. Misfortunes. Secrets.
All preserved within the memory of a silent witness.

Perhaps they were merely superstitions.
Or perhaps they were not.

What is certain is that most of the people of Drywind avoided spending too much time inside that shop.

Many truly believed that the objects gathered within the auction house were... something more than mere old things.

::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 2 – Dahg the Shopkeeper
:::


The owner of the shop was a fifty-year-old man named Dahg.
He lived alone in the rooms above the store.

He had no wife, no children, and no other family. Few visitors ever called on him except for matters related to the business.

He was a reserved man of few words and simple habits. He attended the village celebrations only when necessary and seldom lingered in long conversations outside the taverns or around the wells. He preferred the quiet company of his ledgers, the order of his shelves, and the silent presence of the objects that filled his shop.

Yet those who knew him well understood that beneath his distant manner was an honest man.

Dahg remained patient when others lost their temper.
He listened before passing judgment.
He kept his word even when doing so worked against his own interests.

He paid his debts on time and honored every agreement he made, whether great or small.
If a widow came to sell an old tool, Dahg offered her a fair price.
If a traveler forgot a purse of coins upon the counter, he would find it untouched when he returned to claim it.
And when disputes arose between neighbors over the value of some object, it was not uncommon for them to seek the shopkeeper's opinion, trusting that his judgment would be impartial.

For that reason, he was respected.

But not necessarily understood.
For the auction house exerted a strange influence upon the imagination of the villagers.

Many people in Drywind had crossed the shop's threshold only once or twice in their entire lives. Some avoided it altogether.
The place filled them with a discomfort that was difficult to explain.
And over time, part of that feeling came to cling to its owner as well.
Not because Dahg had ever done anything wrong.
Nor because there were any rumors about him.
It was simply that people tended to distrust what they did not understand.

And for many villagers, it was difficult to separate the man from the collection of strange objects, forgotten relics, and mysterious antiques that filled the auction house.

So when they saw Dahg walking through the streets of the village, they greeted him with respect.
But more often than not, they waited until he had turned the corner before continuing their conversations.

::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 3 – Business
:::


Life in Drywind moved at a slow pace.

The days resembled one another, and the seasons arrived with dependable regularity. Neighbors had known each other for years. Children grew up in the same dirt roads where their parents had once played. Travelers were so rare that the arrival of a stranger could become the subject of conversation for days.

For Dahg, that tranquility had its advantages.
But it also had its drawbacks.

Master Dahg's Auction House was an unusual establishment, and its finest items rarely found buyers among the villagers. 

Most customers came looking for used tools, simple furniture, or household goods. Yet the most valuable pieces in his collection—ancient relics, ornate furnishings, strange instruments, and objects from distant lands—were beyond the means of nearly everyone in Drywind.

His true customers lived in Prosperous City.
Nobles, wealthy merchants, scholars, and collectors who appreciated rare curiosities and were willing to pay handsomely for them.

As a result, Dahg had become increasingly dependent on trade with the city.
Fortunately, over the past few years he had managed to save a considerable amount of money.
And only a few months earlier, he had made the most important investment of his life.

A new wagon.

It was a magnificent freight wagon, built by the finest craftsmen in the region. Sturdy, spacious, and far more reliable than the old wagon he had used for years.
To anyone else, it might have seemed like nothing more than a tool of the trade.
To Dahg, it was something far more important.
It represented the future.

With it, he would be able to transport more valuable goods, travel to more distant towns, and establish new trade routes. It was the centerpiece of a plan he had spent years carefully preparing.
But it was not the only change on the horizon.

Long ago, he had inherited a parcel of land south of Drywind. Land he had never used and was now preparing to sell.
The transaction was already being handled by a scribe in Prosperous City.

Everything seemed to be proceeding exactly as planned.
The documents were ready. The agreements were nearly finalized.
Dahg had even purchased passage on a riverboat that would carry him downstream to the city in just a few days.

If all went well, the sale of the land would provide the capital he needed to expand the business and transform the auction house into something far more profitable.
For the first time in many years, the future seemed promising.

As he positioned an old suit of armor against one wall of the great hall, he allowed himself a smile.
Things were beginning to change.
And this time, they would change for the better.

That was when the shop door opened.

Dahg looked up.
An unfamiliar woman had just entered.
She did not look like one of his usual customers.

Before crossing the threshold, she had lingered for a few moments outside, watching the street. She looked one way, then the other, as though making certain no one had followed her.
Only then had she stepped inside.

She moved quickly through the shadows of the shop, clutching a bundle wrapped in dark cloth.

There was something unusual about her.
Something difficult to explain.

She did not seem frightened.
She seemed uneasy.
Like someone eager to rid herself of something as quickly as possible.

When she reached the counter, she studied Dahg for several seconds.

&nbsp;


Then she carefully placed the bundle upon the wood and said:

— I have something that may interest you.

## Chapter 2 – A Unique Piece


::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 1 – A Reading Lens
:::

::: {custom-style="ChapterStart"}
Dahg gestured toward a worktable at the back of the shop.
&nbsp; &nbsp;
— Over here, if you please.
&nbsp; &nbsp;
The woman nodded and followed him through the narrow aisles formed by shelves and towering stacks of antiques.
:::

Now that he could observe her more closely, Dahg realized she was no ordinary customer.

She appeared to be around forty years old. She wore an elegant traveling coat made from fine fabrics and a pair of impeccably maintained leather gloves. Every detail of her appearance spoke of refinement.

She did not seem like someone in need.
Nor someone accustomed to selling her possessions.
The way she walked, spoke, and observed her surroundings revealed the upbringing of someone from the affluent circles of Prosperous City.

When she reached the table, she carefully set down a package wrapped in dark leather.

For a moment, she remained still, as if hesitating.
Then she loosened the straps and slowly unfolded the covering.

Dahg felt his interest awaken immediately.
Before him appeared an extraordinary object.

Its main component was a large hemispherical lens of perfectly transparent crystal, so clean and polished that it seemed to contain still water within. Its surface caught the candlelight in soft reflections.
The crystal rested upon an elaborate circular frame of ebony and ivory.
The wood had been crafted by skilled hands.
Delicate engravings covered the entire structure, forming intricate geometric patterns intertwined with symbols that Dahg could not identify.
Small gemstones had been set into various points of the frame, adding flashes of color that further enhanced the beauty of the piece.

It was a genuine work of art.

And a remarkable feat of craftsmanship.
Dahg did not need to examine it closely to understand that such a piece must have cost a fortune.

The woman noticed the merchant's expression and allowed herself a faint smile.

— It is not a decorative object, —she explained—. Although most people believe it is.

She placed an open book upon the table and set the device on top of it.

Only then did Dahg notice a detail he had overlooked.
The base contained a circular opening that allowed the flat surface of the crystal to rest directly upon the page.
The woman slowly moved the instrument across the text.

And the letters grew.

The tiny lines of writing appeared enlarged within the crystal with astonishing clarity.
Every letter seemed sharper. More distinct. Easier to read.
The effect was so remarkable that, for a moment, it was difficult to believe there was no hidden trick involved.

— It is a reading lens, —said the woman—. It was created to assist scribes, scholars, and copyists in their work.

Dahg studied the instrument with renewed interest.
Now he saw more than a beautiful object.
He saw a valuable tool. A sophisticated instrument.

A rare combination of art, science, and practical utility.

And yet, as he watched the reflections dancing within the crystal, he could not shake a strange feeling.

There was something about the object.
Something difficult to define.

A quality unlike anything he had encountered in all the years he had spent handling rare and unusual items.
As though the lens had been created to do more than merely enlarge the words printed upon a page.

As though it concealed a purpose it had not yet revealed.

For beyond its decorative beauty, seeing it in use gave it an almost magical quality.

::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 2 – The Purchase
:::



Dahg continued examining the lens for several minutes.
He studied it from different angles, inspected the engravings on its base, and carefully ran his fingers over the ebony and ivory.

The piece appeared to be old.

It showed no significant damage, yet the wear visible in certain places revealed that it had passed through many hands over the years. In the deepest recesses of the engravings, traces of dark dust still remained, left there by the slow passage of time.

— Do you know who made it? —Dahg asked.

The woman shook her head.

— No.

— And where does it come from?

— I don't know that either.

The answer surprised the merchant.
The woman seemed to notice.

— I bought it about a year ago at an antiques market in Prosperous City. The seller didn't know its origin either. According to him, it had belonged to another dealer before he acquired it. And that dealer had obtained it from someone else.

Dahg nodded slowly.

That was not as unusual as it might sound.
Many old objects found their way into auction houses after passing through countless owners.

Yet in this case, the situation was particularly striking.
An item so unusual would normally be accompanied by stories, documents, or records that allowed at least part of its history to be traced.

This one, however, seemed to have left behind nothing but a chain of nameless owners.
As though it had been passed from hand to hand for years.
As though no one had kept it long enough to discover where it had come from.

Dahg looked at the lens once more.
The longer he studied it, the more convinced he became that he was looking at an exceptional piece.
The quality of the materials.
The precision of the crystal.
The complexity of the engravings.
Everything suggested it had been crafted by extraordinary artisans.

And that meant only one thing.

It was worth a great deal of money.
Perhaps more than he could afford at that moment.
Even so, he decided to try.

— How much are you asking for it?

The woman answered immediately.

— Ten coins.

Her tone was firm and final.
As though the decision had been made long before she entered the shop.

Dahg remained silent for several seconds.
He had spent his entire life buying and selling things.
He understood the rules of a negotiation perfectly well.
The seller named a price.
The buyer offered another.
They argued.
They bargained.
They searched for common ground.
That was how such matters worked.
But not this time.
The amount was absurdly low.
It was nowhere near the true value of the piece.

For a moment, he wondered whether the woman simply did not realize what she was selling.

Yet her manner, her education, and the way she had presented the object ruled out that possibility.
She knew exactly what she had.
And yet she was willing to sell it for a fraction of its worth.

Dahg asked no further questions.
He walked to the room behind the counter.
There he unlocked a small iron strongbox.
He removed a leather pouch.
And returned to the table.

Carefully, he counted out ten coins.
One by one.

Then he placed them before the woman.
She barely glanced at them.
She gathered them up immediately.
Put the money away.
And rose to her feet.

— It has been a pleasure doing business with you, Master Dahg.

— The pleasure is mine, madam.

The woman inclined her head slightly.
Then she turned toward the door.
And left.

Dahg watched her through the window as she walked away.
He expected to see her stop at one of the market stalls or exchange words with an acquaintance.
But she did neither.

She crossed the street at a brisk pace.
Then, rounding the corner of the neighboring building, disappeared into the crowd.
As though she had been eager to leave.
As though the true purpose of her visit had not been to sell the lens.

But to be rid of it.

::: {custom-style="PartTitle"}
Part 3 – A Closer Examination
:::


As soon as the woman left the shop, Dahg turned his full attention back to the strange reading lens.

He placed it on the counter and began examining it carefully.
Now that it belonged to him, he could study every detail at his leisure.

The ebony-and-ivory base was just as impressive as it had been at first sight. Yet upon closer inspection, he discovered small cracks in parts of the wood and signs of wear along the more prominent carvings.

Nothing serious.
A little cleaning.
Some wax.
Perhaps a light polishing.

With a few minor repairs, it would recover much of its former splendor.
And then he could sell it for a considerable sum.

Dahg smiled.
He had made an excellent purchase.
Opportunities like that came along only a handful of times in a lifetime.

At that moment, the bell above the door rang.
A customer had entered the shop looking for carpentry tools.
Dahg set the lens aside and returned to his duties.

Then more visitors arrived.
A farmer interested in a lamp.
An elderly woman searching for an iron pot.
A traveler hoping to sell a bundle of blankets.
And before he realized it, afternoon had given way to night.

By the time he finally closed the shop and extinguished the last candles in the main hall, he was tired.

He slowly climbed the stairs leading to his rooms.
He was about to go to bed when he remembered the lens.
For some reason, he did not want the day to end without looking at it one more time.
He went back for it.
And carried it with him to his bedroom.

The room was simple.
A bed.
A wardrobe.
A worktable.
Several shelves crowded with books and documents.

Dahg placed the lens upon the table and pulled an oil lamp closer.

Then he picked up his ledger.
If the instrument had truly been designed to make reading easier, there could be no better test than a long list of numbers.
He carefully rested the flat surface of the crystal upon one of the pages.
And looked.

The figures appeared enlarged with astonishing clarity.
Every stroke.
Every line.
Every tiny detail of the handwriting.
Everything seemed sharper.
Easier to distinguish.

Dahg smiled with satisfaction.
The woman had told the truth.
It was a magnificent tool.

He slowly moved the lens across different pages of the ledger, reviewing old entries.
And then something strange happened.

At first, he thought it was merely a reflection.

A faint mist appeared within the crystal.
It was barely visible.
A thin, whitish cloud that seemed to drift beneath the surface.

The mist began to spread.
Growing denser.
More opaque.
Until it completely obscured the writing beneath.

The merchant lifted the lens from the book.
He held it up to the light.
He saw no cracks.
No moisture.
No defect of any kind.
And yet the mist remained.

He turned the object over.
Tilted it.
Held it closer to the lamp.

Nothing changed.

For a moment, he thought it might be a reflection coming from the window.

He rose from his chair and drew the curtain shut.
Then he returned.

The mist was still floating within the crystal.
And now it seemed to be moving.
Dahg felt a faint chill run through him.
He moved the lamp farther away.
Then brought it closer again.
Once more, he examined the surface.
That was when he noticed something that made his heart skip a beat.

Shapes were beginning to emerge within the white clouds.
Shadows.
Tiny figures that seemed to move slowly.
As though he were watching something taking place far away.
As though the crystal had become a window.

Unable to understand what he was seeing, Dahg leaned forward.
Closer and closer.
Until his face was only inches from the sphere.

And at last, he was able to make out a human figure.
A figure that seemed disturbingly familiar.
